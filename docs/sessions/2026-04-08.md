# Session Log — April 8, 2026

## Goal

Build Phase 4 of Basira: expand from 3 Sentinel-2 time points to monthly
temporal resolution for Riyadh, covering January 2020 through April 2026.
The existing prototype had annual snapshots (2020, 2023, 2026); Phase 4
makes it possible to see change month by month.

---

## Step 1: Catalog Dry-Run

We started by querying the Sentinel Hub Catalog API — free, no processing
units consumed — to understand what data existed before committing to any
downloads. Created `src/phase4_catalog.py`.

Results: **950 Sentinel-2 L2A scenes** over the Riyadh bounding box
(24.55-24.85°N, 46.55-46.95°E), spanning 76 months. Cloud cover was
overwhelmingly low — Riyadh is desert. 700 of 950 scenes had < 10% cloud
cover. Every single month had at least one usable scene (< 30% cloud).
Most months had a best scene at 0.0% cloud.

PU estimation: the Sentinel Hub formula `(w*h)/(512²) × (bands/3)` predicted
1,305.5 PU for 76 requests at 2002×1687 pixels with 4 bands. This is ~4.4%
of the 30K/month free tier. We set a safety ceiling at 5,000 PU.

Key deliverables:
- `outputs/phase4_catalog.csv` — full 950-scene catalog
- `outputs/phase4_catalog_summary.txt` — monthly summary

## Step 2: Download

Created `src/phase4_download.py`. For each of the 76 months, it downloads the
single best scene (lowest cloud cover) as a 4-band GeoTIFF (B02, B03, B04, B08)
at 20m resolution in UTM Zone 38N, with DEFLATE compression.

The script is idempotent — skips files that already exist — so it's safe to
re-run after interruption. Progress logging shows scene number, cloud cover,
download status, file size, and cumulative PU.

Credential handling was initially fragile (env vars on the command line). We
fixed this by adding `python-dotenv` support: credentials in `.env` (gitignored),
with `.env.example` committed as a template. All three scripts that touch
Sentinel Hub (`download_optical.py`, `phase4_catalog.py`, `phase4_download.py`)
now call `load_dotenv()` at import time.

Total download: 76 GeoTIFFs, ~1 GB, stored in `data/processed/monthly/`.

## Step 3: Quicklook Grid and NDVI

Created `src/phase4_quicklook.py` — builds a year × month thumbnail grid of
all 76 scenes with percentile-stretched RGB. This immediately revealed that
about 10 scenes had a diagonal nodata strip where the Sentinel-2 swath only
partially covered the AOI.

Created `src/phase4_ndvi.py` — computes NDVI = (B08-B04)/(B08+B04) for each
monthly scene and saves single-band float32 rasters. Initial whole-AOI mean
NDVI was reported but turned out to be misleading (see below).

## Step 4: Valid Mask — First Attempt (Bug)

Created `src/phase4_valid_mask.py` with a strict logical AND across all 76
scenes: a pixel was "valid" only if it had data in every single scene. This
was far too aggressive — the 10 partial-coverage scenes collectively wiped out
the entire western half of the AOI. Result: only 42.9% valid pixels, which is
useless.

**Fix:** replaced strict AND with a temporal-coverage threshold. For each pixel,
we count how many of the 76 scenes have valid data, then threshold at 80%
(pixel must be valid in >= 61 of 76 scenes). This brought coverage up to ~92%,
matching the visual reality from the quicklook grid.

Also added `outputs/phase4_pixel_coverage.tif` — a per-pixel count of valid
scenes — as a diagnostic raster, with a viridis heatmap visualization.

The 10 partial-coverage scenes (all < 25 MB vs. ~40 MB for full scenes):
2020_04, 2020_06, 2020_07, 2021_03, 2021_09, 2021_12, 2022_07, 2023_11,
2024_01, 2026_04.

## Step 5: ROI Definitions

Created `src/phase4_rois.py` defining 4 regions of interest:

1. **Wadi Hanifa** (24.62-24.78°N, 46.58-46.65°E) — the wadi corridor, a
   known greening area from Riyadh's rehabilitation projects.
2. **King Salman Park** (24.80-24.85°N, 46.55-46.62°E) — major construction
   zone on the NW edge.
3. **Northern Expansion** (24.80-24.85°N, 46.65-46.75°E) — new suburban
   grading zones.
4. **Central Urban** (24.66-24.71°N, 46.68-46.74°E) — established dense city,
   control baseline.

The initial Wadi Hanifa bbox was too large (covered most of central Riyadh,
not the actual wadi corridor). Tightened it, and adjusted Central Urban to
avoid overlap.

Outputs: GeoJSON (WGS84), pixel-space windows (JSON for downstream scripts),
and an overlay PNG on a recent RGB scene for visual sanity checking.

## Step 6: NDVI Time Series — First Attempt (Bug)

Created `src/phase4_ndvi_timeseries.py`. First run produced N/A for every
ROI-month because the broken valid mask (42.9% coverage) left zero valid
pixels in most ROIs. After fixing the mask to 80% threshold, the script
produced real numbers.

Results showed negative NDVI trends in all 4 ROIs (−0.001 to −0.002/yr) with
strong seasonal oscillation. Mean NDVI ranged from ~0.05 to ~0.12, typical
for a mostly-desert environment. The signal was dominated by seasonal
variation (summer heat suppresses even sparse vegetation) rather than any
long-term trend.

Central Urban showed an anomalous spike to NDVI ~0.20 in 2022-07.

## Step 7: Anomaly Investigation

Created `src/phase4_anomaly_check.py` to z-score each ROI's monthly NDVI
and flag |z| > 3. The Central Urban 2022-07 spike was confirmed as an
outlier. Root cause: the 2022_07 scene is one of the 10 partial-coverage
scenes (21 MB vs. ~40 MB normal). It clipped the Central Urban ROI to ~20
valid pixels, and those few pixels happened to include a vegetated patch,
inflating the mean.

**Fix:** added a `MIN_VALID_PIXELS = 500` threshold to the time-series script.
Any ROI-month with fewer than 500 valid pixels records NaN instead of a
spurious mean. This eliminated the spike without affecting the 60+ months
that have full coverage.

## Step 8: Deseasonalization

Modified the time-series script to compute a per-calendar-month climatology
(mean NDVI for each Jan, Feb, ..., Dec across all years) and subtract it to
get the anomaly. Trend is now fit on both raw NDVI and deseasonalized anomaly.

New output: `outputs/phase4_ndvi_anomaly.png` — 4-panel plot showing anomaly
centered on zero with trend line overlaid.

## Step 9: Pixel-Level Greening Map

Created `src/phase4_green_map.py`. Instead of relying on mean NDVI (which is
insensitive in arid environments), this computes per-pixel "vegetation
persistence" — the fraction of months where NDVI > 0.20 — for 2020 and 2025
separately, then classifies:

- **Stable green:** persistence > 0.6 in both years
- **New green:** persistence < 0.2 in 2020 AND > 0.6 in 2025
- **Lost green:** persistence > 0.6 in 2020 AND < 0.2 in 2025

This is much more informative than mean NDVI for detecting localized greening
in an environment where most pixels are bare soil.

## Step 10: Web Export

Created `src/phase4_export_web.py`. Produces web-ready files in
`webapp/data/phase4/` without modifying any existing webapp code:

- `green_map.png` — transparent RGBA overlay, reprojected to WGS84
- `green_map_bounds.json` — bounding box for Leaflet's L.imageOverlay
- `latest_rgb.png` + bounds — most recent full-coverage scene
- `ndvi_timeseries.json` — per-ROI time series, NaN entries skipped
- `rois.geojson` — ROI polygons (already WGS84)

Wiring these into the Leaflet app is Phase 4.5.

## What we learned

1. **The PU formula is reliable.** Estimated 1,305.5 PU, actual consumption
   matched. Safe to budget multi-city expansion based on catalog dry-runs.

2. **Strict AND masks are too aggressive.** Any per-scene nodata — even from
   swath geometry — compounds across months. A coverage-threshold approach
   (80%) is much more practical.

3. **Mean NDVI in arid environments is a weak signal.** Baseline is 0.05-0.10,
   seasonal amplitude ±0.03, and any real greening is tiny against this. Pixel-
   level persistence metrics (NDVI > threshold for N months) detect localized
   change that whole-ROI means cannot.

4. **Partial-coverage scenes create artifacts.** When a scene clips an ROI to
   a handful of pixels, the mean becomes unrepresentative. A minimum-pixel
   threshold (500 px) is essential for robust time-series statistics.

5. **Deseasonalization is necessary but not sufficient.** Removing the seasonal
   cycle clarifies the trend, but the remaining inter-annual noise is still
   large relative to any real greening signal. The pixel-level green map is
   a stronger indicator.
